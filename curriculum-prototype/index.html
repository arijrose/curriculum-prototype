Lesson 3: Picking a Community Challenge and Vision Brainstorming

▧ Lesson Plan Grades: 9–12 Time: 120 minutes Collection: Unit or Curriculum Name

[Lesson 3] guides youth to choose one of the top five community challenges they identified to focus on for the remainder of the program. They will also participate in vision brainstorming regarding changes they hope to see related to the community challenge. Lastly, youth will learn about mindfulness strategies that can be used when they feel stressed/overwhelmed, such as when working on a community challenge.

Session Objectives

In this session, students will:

  • Pick a community challenge to focus on in the program
  • Brainstorm a vision for change related to the community challenge
  • Learn a mindfulness technique

Rationale

It is essential for youth to not only pick the community challenge to focus on but also to have time to envision change related to that challenge, which may help with building hope that will motivate the group to continue working on the challenge. The group must consider the time and effort required to address different problems and individual versus team needs. Additionally, after choosing the community challenge in this session, youth may feel nervous or overwhelmed with managing community change. Therefore, the focus on learning mindfulness strategies is intended to equip the youth with a tool that they can use to help them in the process.

Brief Session Agenda

  1. Opening and Mindful Check-In (10 minutes)
  2. Icebreaker – Apples and Oranges (10 minutes)
  3. Metaphorical Mindfulness Exercise: Mindful Seaweed (15 minutes)
  4. Picking a Community Challenge (22 minutes)
  5. Break (10 minutes)
  6. Vision Brainstorming (45 minutes)
  7. Students Check-Out Notes Completion (5 minutes)
  8. Closing (3 minutes)

General Session-Related Tips

  • Remember to reinforce the idea of short-term vs. long-term involvement: When youth pick a community challenge to focus on for the remainder of the program, it will be essential to remind them of the time they have to work on an action in the program. Facilitators should encourage youth to think about feasibility given the timeline, especially emphasizing short-term vs. long-term involvement for enacting change for a specific topic.
  • Center youth voice: The main goal is for facilitators to hear youth perspectives as they pick a community challenge and discuss their visions of change. Facilitators should listen actively, reflect and validate youth perspectives, and create a space for diverse youth voices.

    Icebreaker – Apples and Oranges (10 minutes)

    Objective

    To build trust within the group.

    Materials

    Note: These will be linked

    • None.

    Source

    Note: These will be linked

    • Institute for Community Research (2014) – YPAR Curriculum [Module 2, Section 1, Unit 4, Activity 2: Trust activities, Apples and Oranges]
    • https://www.oregon.gov/oha/PH/HEALTHYPEOPLEFAMILIES/YOUTH/Documents/Curric ulumYPAR2014.pdf

    Preparation

    None

    Transition (1 minute)

    Say “Let’s start today with an activity that will help us think more about working together as a team.”

    Description

    Have all participants join hands in a circle. Have the participants alternate calling out “apple” or “orange” around the circle. There must be an even number of people to do this activity so that no two “apples” or “oranges” are next to each other. Next, ask the apples to lean slowly while the oranges bend backward, ensuring they continue holding hands tightly. People need to trust slowly and carefully, and when relying as far as they can, they should depend on the support of the people on either side to keep their balance. Have everyone return to an upright position, then have the groups switch positions. This time, have the apples lean backward while the oranges lean forward.

    Debrief: [Script] (1 minute):

    Ask “Though it does not seem like people would be able to maintain their positions, it works very well when the activity is done carefully. This activity demonstrates the power of people working together to achieve something that would not be possible alone.”

    Metaphorical Mindfulness Exercise: Mindful Seaweed (15 minutes)

    Objective

    For youth to get a feel for what acting mindfully looks like and how to connect mindfulness to their lived experience and the content in the curriculum. The mindful seaweed activity aims to help youth be flexible and resilient when they come across challenging situations. Since youth will pick a community challenge in this session, stress and negative emotions may arise if they do not get their top choice. Therefore, it will require them to be flexible like seaweed.

    Materials

    Note: These will be linked

    • Visual aids (LEAP Facilitator Handout Manual)
    • Example script for the Mindful Seaweed exercise (LEAP Facilitator Mindfulness Manual).

    Source

    Note: These will be linked

    • Developed for intervention.

    Preparation

    Review the example script for the exercise and practice leading it using your own words, details, and phrasing that will connect with the youth. You will be asked to provide a personal experience connecting your daily life to when you were mindful or mindless. This example will help you think through ahead of time.

    Transition (1 minute)

    Say “Now we are going to transition to a metaphorical mindfulness exercise. Remember, these metaphors are intended to help us understand how we can act mindfully in our daily lives, both inside and outside of our sessions. First, we’ll take some time to think about the metaphor. Then we’ll discuss how it relates to what we’ve discussed together.”

    Description

    This activity has two main parts: (1) leading the metaphorical exercise and (2) facilitating discussion regarding how the metaphor connects to the content covered in the curriculum so far.

    1. Leading the Mindful Seaweed Exercise. (3 minutes)
      • Facilitators will introduce the metaphorical exercise in their own words, focusing on how mindfulness helps us be flexible and resilient when stressful or challenging things happen outside our control.
      • Lead the metaphorical exercise. Show the appropriate visual aids and lead the exercise in your own words (see the example script in the Facilitator Mindfulness Manual).
    2. Facilitate discussion regarding how the metaphor connects to the content covered in the curriculum so far (10 minutes):
      • Facilitators will make comments and ask questions to prompt the youth to connect to their daily lives and, specifically, to the content of the curriculum so far. Example comments and questions include (but are not limited to) the following:
        • As the facilitator, share a brief, relevant personal experience that connects elements of the metaphor to a time when you acted mindfully or mindlessly. Clearly emphasize how the elements of the metaphor map onto your experience.
        • Who can share an example of when they experienced [ENTER SPECIFIC ELEMENTS OF METAPHOR HERE] in your real life? What was it like? How did you act? What did you learn from this about being mindful?
        • Can anyone think of a time when we have experienced [ENTER SPECIFIC ELEMENTS OF METAPHOR HERE] during our sessions, with our group, her together? What was it like? How did we act? What can we learn from this about being mindful?
        • How does [ENTER SPECIFIC ELEMENTS OF METAPHOR HERE] relate to what we discussed earlier today? Or the last time we met? What can we learn from this about being mindful when engaging with our communities or stepping up as leaders?

    Debrief: [Script] (1 minute):

    Ask “Thank you for thinking about this metaphor and engaging in discussion about how it relates to what we’ve been talking about together. We will talk about this metaphor again in the future. We will also talk about other metaphors that can help us understand how to act mindfully in our work. Let’s keep building our mindfulness skills—to help us be strong, persistent, and flexible in this work.”

      • Facilitators can lead a discussion with the youth in which they discuss how the community challenge impacts the health of youth and the community's health. Potential questions that facilitators can ask:

    Vision Brainstorming (45 minutes)

    Objective

    To brainstorm visions for change related to the selected community challenge.

    Materials

    Note: These will be linked

    • Handout: What Change Do You Hope To See Related To [The Community Challenge]
    • Large easel paper
    • Collage materials (pictures, magazines, scissors (optional), glue/tape)
    • Markers and pens
    • Paper
    • Short-term projects samples (2)
    • Handout: Term plan sample.

    Source

    Note: These will be linked

    • Developed for intervention; adapted from Community Tool Box – https://ctb.ku.edu/en/table-of-contents/structure/strategic-planning/vision-mission- statements/main

    Preparation

    Set up three areas in the room (one for each of the different types of brainstorming groups). Facilitators can write out the vision brainstorming question on a large easel paper visible to all groups (i.e., “What is the change that you hope to see related to [the community challenge]?”)

    Transition (1 minute)

    Say “Since you have chosen the community challenge that we will be focusing on for the rest of the program and started talking about the importance of that issue, let’s start thinking about 1) What change do you hope to see related to that community challenge?; 2) What support do you need?; 3) What activities need to be integrated to plan ahead of time? Here is a sample of a term plan.”

    Description

    In this activity, facilitators will be working with youth to start thinking about the change they hope to see related to the community challenge – which we will call a “vision” and its challenges. Refer to the short-term project samples (2) discussed in the previous activity. This activity has two parts: 1) vision brainstorming in 3 small groups and 2) large group share back.

    1. Vision Brainstorming (23 minutes):
      • Facilitators will set up three options for youth and ask them to split up based on which of the three possibilities interests them. Facilitators can ask if anyone is willing to switch groups if there is an unequal split. The three groups are 1) verbal brainstorming, 2) visual brainstorming, and 3) written brainstorming. All three groups work at the same time.
        1. Small Group Option 1: Verbal Brainstorm: Ask if one youth can volunteer to take notes and if another can present the ideas at the end of the discussion. Ask the group the following question to help brainstorm thoughts about what they see as the vision: “What is the change that you hope to see related to [the community challenge]?” The youth can discuss the vision, and one person (or a facilitator) can help take notes.
        2. Small Group Option 2: Visual Brainstorm: Facilitators will provide collage materials. Facilitators will ask the group to use the collage materials to describe a vision: “What change do you hope to see related to [the community challenge]?
        3. Small Group Option 3: Written Brainstorm: Ask the group the following question to help brainstorm thoughts about what they see as the vision: “What is the change that you hope to see related to [the community challenge]?” The youth should write their visions on paper. Once they’re done writing, they can share their notes.
    2. Large Group Share Back (20 minutes)
      • Facilitators will ask each small group to share their ideas with the larger group. Remind the groups not presenting to listen to the small group presenting. One facilitator can moderate the share back while the other facilitator can note the ideas that the youth share on large easel paper to capture everyone’s ideas and potentially group together similar ideas.

    Debrief: [Script] (1 minute):

    Ask “You are all doing such great and important work – starting to think about the change you hope to see is a step forward in figuring out how to be involved. We want to emphasize that in this program, we will be working together to figure out what we can do as a group during the time we have in the program to contribute to the vision. We will be returning to this discussion later in the program.”
